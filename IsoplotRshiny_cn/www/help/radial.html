Radial plots are bivariate (xi,yi) scatterplots where:

xi = 1∕σ(zi) and yi = (zi - zo)∕σ(zi) (for 1 ≤ i ≤ n)

with zi a transformation of some data and σ(zi) the corresponding measurement uncertainty. For example, if zi = log(ti) then σ(zi) = σ(ti)∕ti. zo is a convenient central value such as the weighted mean. The slope of a line connecting the origin (x=0,y=0) of a radial plot with a data point (xi,yi) equals zi, and the horizontal distance along x-axis is a measure if its precision. Thus, the radial plot simultaneously visualises a measurement’s value and precision. No other graphical method achieves this goal with the same elegance (Galbraith, 1988). This makes the radial plot the method of choice for visualising heteroscedastic data, i.e. data with variable measurement uncertainties. Traditional applications in the Earth Sciences are fission track and luminescence dating (e.g., Galbraith, 1990; Galbraith et al., 1999). IsoplotR generalises it to all other geochronometers as well.

Legend:

  1. central age = t ± x | y

    where:

    t: the central age, which is defined as eμ where μ is the mean of the following log-normal distribution:
    ti ~ Norm(μ, s[ti]2 + ω2) (eq. 1)
    with s[ti] the standard error of the ith (out of n) measurements, and ω the overdispersion.
    x: the estimated standard deviation of t, estimated by the method of maximum likelihood.
    y: the 100(1-α)% confidence interval for t.
  2. MSWD = m, p(χ2) = p

    where:

    m: the Mean Square of the Weighted Deviates a.k.a. the reduced Chi-squared statistic. MSWD values greater than unity suggest that the dataset under consideration is overdispersed with respect to the analyical uncertainties.
    p: the p-value of a Chi-square probability for homogeneity. High values of p (>0.05, say) indicate that there is not enough evidence for overdispersion.
  3. dispersion = ω + u /- l %

    where:

    ω: the dispersion defined by eq. 1.
    u: width of the upper half of a (1-α)% confidence interval for ω.
    l: width of the lower half of the (1-α)% confidence interval for ω.
